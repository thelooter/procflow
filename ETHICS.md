# Ethical use

procflow is licensed under `MIT OR Apache-2.0` (see [`LICENSE-MIT`](./LICENSE-MIT)
and [`LICENSE-APACHE`](./LICENSE-APACHE)). Those licenses place **no restriction**
on who may use procflow or for what, and **this file does not change that**.
Nothing here is a license term, a condition, or a legal obligation — it is a
request from the author, appealing to your judgment.

## The request

procflow attributes network traffic to individual processes and the people
running them. That makes it a capable monitoring tool, and monitoring tools can
be turned toward surveillance. The author asks that you **not** use procflow:

- for **military** purposes, or
- for **government mass surveillance** of people.

## What this is *not* meant to discourage

Ordinary and dual-use cases are entirely fine — including:

- personal, hobbyist, research, and educational use;
- organizations (companies, universities, public institutions) measuring the
  bandwidth of **their own** infrastructure and services;
- procflow being embedded in a broader product that **also serves consumers**.

The concern is the deliberate use of procflow to surveil or harm people — not its
incidental presence in a general-purpose or dual-use product.

## Why a request, and not a license clause

A license that restricted *who* may use the software would no longer be
open source (it fails the OSI Open Source Definition and the FSF's "freedom 0"):
it could not ship in Linux distributions, would be rejected by most organizations'
policies — turning away legitimate users — and would be effectively unenforceable
by a solo author anyway. Keeping procflow freely usable while stating this
preference plainly is the honest trade. The full reasoning is in
[`docs/adr/0012-dual-license-mit-or-apache-bpf-dual-mit-gpl.md`](./docs/adr/0012-dual-license-mit-or-apache-bpf-dual-mit-gpl.md).

Thank you for respecting this.
